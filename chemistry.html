‚öõÔ∏è Basic Notes on Atomic Structure

  • Atoms: The smallest units of matter, consisting of a nucleus and electrons.
  • Protons: Positively charged particles found in the nucleus. üî¥ Relative charge: +1, Relative mass: 1.
  • Neutrons: Neutral particles in the nucleus. ‚ö™ Relative charge: 0, Relative mass: 1.
  • Electrons: Negatively charged particles orbiting the nucleus. üîµ Relative charge: -1, Relative mass: ~0.
  • Atomic Number (Z): The number of protons in an atom. It defines the element.
  • Mass Number (A): The total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus.
  • Electronic Configuration: Describes how electrons are arranged in shells. e.g., Oxygen = 2,6.
  • Isotopes: Atoms of the same element with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons.

üß™ Periodic Table ‚Äì Key Points

  • Groups: Vertical columns. Elements in the same group have similar chemical properties and the same number of outer electrons.
  • Periods: Horizontal rows. Each new period represents a new electron shell.
  • Metals vs Non-Metals: Metals are on the left and centre; non-metals are on the right. Metals tend to lose electrons, non-metals gain them.
  • Group 1 ‚Äì Alkali Metals: Soft, reactive metals. Reactivity increases down the group.
  • Group 7 ‚Äì Halogens: Very reactive non-metals. Reactivity decreases down the group.
  • Group 0 ‚Äì Noble Gases: Unreactive gases with full outer shells. They are inert and stable.
  • Transition Metals: Found in the middle block. They form coloured compounds and can have multiple oxidation states.

üîó Bonding and Structure

  • Ionic Bonding: Between metals and non-metals. Electrons are transferred from metal to non-metal to form ions. Strong electrostatic forces hold ions in a lattice.
  • Covalent Bonding: Between non-metals. Atoms share electrons to achieve full outer shells. Found in molecules like H‚ÇÇO, CO‚ÇÇ, and O‚ÇÇ.
  • Metallic Bonding: In metals. Positive metal ions surrounded by a sea of delocalised electrons. Explains conductivity and malleability.
  • Simple Molecular Structures: Low melting/boiling points due to weak intermolecular forces (e.g., H‚ÇÇ, CH‚ÇÑ, H‚ÇÇO).
  • Giant Covalent Structures: High melting points and hardness (e.g., diamond, graphite, silicon dioxide).
  • Polymers: Long chains of repeating units. Properties depend on the forces between the chains (e.g., poly(ethene)).
  • Alloys: Mixtures of metals. Disrupt regular arrangement to make materials harder (e.g., steel, bronze).
