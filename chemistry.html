‚öõÔ∏è Basic Notes on Atomic Structure

  • Atoms: The smallest units of matter, consisting of a nucleus and electrons.
  • Protons: Positively charged particles found in the nucleus. üî¥ Relative charge: +1, Relative mass: 1.
  • Neutrons: Neutral particles in the nucleus. ‚ö™ Relative charge: 0, Relative mass: 1.
  • Electrons: Negatively charged particles orbiting the nucleus. üîµ Relative charge: -1, Relative mass: ~0.
  • Atomic Number (Z): The number of protons in an atom. It defines the element.
  • Mass Number (A): The total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus.
  • Electronic Configuration: Describes how electrons are arranged in shells. e.g., Oxygen = 2,6.
  • Isotopes: Atoms of the same element with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons.

üß™ Periodic Table ‚Äì Key Points

  • Groups: Vertical columns. Elements in the same group have similar chemical properties and the same number of outer electrons.
  • Periods: Horizontal rows. Each new period represents a new electron shell.
  • Metals vs Non-Metals: Metals are on the left and centre; non-metals are on the right. Metals tend to lose electrons, non-metals gain them.
  • Group 1 ‚Äì Alkali Metals: Soft, reactive metals. Reactivity increases down the group.
  • Group 7 ‚Äì Halogens: Very reactive non-metals. Reactivity decreases down the group.
  • Group 0 ‚Äì Noble Gases: Unreactive gases with full outer shells. They are inert and stable.
  • Transition Metals: Found in the middle block. They form coloured compounds and can have multiple oxidation states.

üîó Bonding and Structure

  • Ionic Bonding: Between metals and non-metals. Electrons are transferred from metal to non-metal to form ions. Strong electrostatic forces hold ions in a lattice.
  • Covalent Bonding: Between non-metals. Atoms share electrons to achieve full outer shells. Found in molecules like H‚ÇÇO, CO‚ÇÇ, and O‚ÇÇ.
  • Metallic Bonding: In metals. Positive metal ions surrounded by a sea of delocalised electrons. Explains conductivity and malleability.
  • Simple Molecular Structures: Low melting/boiling points due to weak intermolecular forces (e.g., H‚ÇÇ, CH‚ÇÑ, H‚ÇÇO).
  • Giant Covalent Structures: High melting points and hardness (e.g., diamond, graphite, silicon dioxide).
  • Polymers: Long chains of repeating units. Properties depend on the forces between the chains (e.g., poly(ethene)).
  • Alloys: Mixtures of metals. Disrupt regular arrangement to make materials harder (e.g., steel, bronze).

‚öñÔ∏è Chemical Equations

  • Word Equations: Show the names of reactants and products (e.g., magnesium + oxygen ‚Üí magnesium oxide).
  • Symbol Equations: Use chemical formulas to represent reactions (e.g., Mg + O‚ÇÇ ‚Üí MgO).
  • Balancing Equations: The number of atoms must be equal on both sides. Only coefficients (big numbers) can be changed.
  • State Symbols: Show physical states: (s) solid, (l) liquid, (g) gas, (aq) aqueous (dissolved in water).
  • Conservation of Mass: No atoms are lost or gained in a chemical reaction ‚Äî total mass of reactants = total mass of products.
  • Ionic Equations: Show the species involved in the actual reaction, removing spectator ions.
  • Precipitation Reactions: A solid (precipitate) forms when two solutions react ‚Äî useful for ionic equations.

üß™ Acids, Bases, and Neutralisation

  • Acids: Substances that release H‚Å∫ ions in solution. pH < 7 (e.g., HCl, H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ, HNO‚ÇÉ).
  • Bases: Substances that neutralise acids. Includes metal oxides, metal hydroxides, and ammonia.
  • Alkalis: Bases that are soluble in water and release OH‚Åª ions (e.g., NaOH, KOH).
  • Neutralisation: Acid + Base ‚Üí Salt + Water. Key reaction for titrations and indicators.
  • Indicators: Substances that change colour with pH (e.g., litmus, methyl orange, phenolphthalein).
  • Strong vs Weak Acids: Strong acids fully ionise (e.g., HCl); weak acids only partially ionise (e.g., ethanoic acid).
  • Titration: Used to find the exact volume of acid or alkali needed to neutralise a solution using a pipette, burette, and indicator.
  • pH Scale: Ranges from 0 (strong acid) to 14 (strong alkali). pH 7 is neutral (pure water).

‚ö° Electrolysis

  • Electrolysis: The process of breaking down an ionic compound using electricity. Only works when the compound is molten or in solution.
  • Electrolyte: The ionic substance being broken down. It must contain free-moving ions to conduct electricity.
  • Electrodes: Conductors through which electricity enters and leaves the electrolyte. Anode (+), Cathode (‚àí).
  • Cathode (‚àí): Positive ions (cations) are attracted and gain electrons (reduction). "Red cat" = Reduction at cathode.
  • Anode (+): Negative ions (anions) are attracted and lose electrons (oxidation). "OIL RIG" = Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain.
  • Molten Compounds: Metal forms at cathode, non-metal at anode (e.g., lead bromide ‚Üí Pb + Br‚ÇÇ).

üî• Energy Changes in Reactions

  • Exothermic Reactions: Transfer energy to the surroundings ‚Äî temperature increases. Examples: combustion, neutralisation, many oxidation reactions.
  • Endothermic Reactions: Take in energy from the surroundings ‚Äî temperature decreases. Examples: thermal decomposition, citric acid + sodium hydrogen carbonate.
  • Reaction Profiles: Diagrams showing energy changes. Exothermic reactions start high and end low; endothermic start low and end high.
  • Activation Energy: The minimum energy required for particles to react. Represented by the energy "hump" on a profile.
  • Bond Breaking: Endothermic ‚Äî energy is absorbed to break bonds.
  • Bond Making: Exothermic ‚Äî energy is released when new bonds form.
  • Energy Change (ŒîH): Can be estimated from bond energies: ŒîH = Energy in ‚àí Energy out.
  • Practical Applications: Exothermic: hand warmers, self-heating cans. Endothermic: cold packs for sports injuries.

‚è±Ô∏è Rates of Reaction

  • Rate of Reaction: How fast reactants are converted into products. Measured by the amount of product formed or reactant used over time.
  • Factors Affecting Rate: Temperature, concentration (or pressure for gases), surface area, and the presence of a catalyst.
  • Collision Theory: Particles must collide with enough energy to react. More frequent or energetic collisions = faster reaction.
  • Temperature: Increases particle energy and frequency of collisions ‚Üí faster rate.
  • Concentration/Pressure: More particles in the same space = more frequent collisions.
  • Surface Area: Breaking solids into smaller pieces exposes more particles to react ‚Üí increases rate.
  • Catalysts: Lower the activation energy needed for a reaction to occur. They are not used up.
  • Required Practical: Measuring the volume of gas produced or the time for a colour change (e.g., magnesium + acid, or sodium thiosulfate + acid).

üåç Earth and Atmospheric Science

  • Earth's Early Atmosphere: Formed by volcanic activity ‚Äî mainly carbon dioxide, with little or no oxygen, and some water vapour and ammonia.
  • Formation of Oceans: Water vapour condensed as Earth cooled, forming oceans and removing CO‚ÇÇ from the atmosphere.
  • Evolution of Oxygen: Algae and plants evolved and began photosynthesis, gradually increasing oxygen levels and reducing CO‚ÇÇ.
  • Modern Atmosphere: ~78% nitrogen, ~21% oxygen, small amounts of argon, carbon dioxide, and water vapour.
  • Greenhouse Gases: Include CO‚ÇÇ, methane, and water vapour. They trap heat in the atmosphere and maintain Earth‚Äôs temperature.
  • Global Climate Change: Linked to increasing levels of greenhouse gases from human activities ‚Äî burning fossil fuels, deforestation, agriculture.
  • Carbon Footprint: The total greenhouse gas emissions caused directly and indirectly by an individual, event, organisation, or product.
  • Air Pollution: Burning fuels releases pollutants like carbon monoxide, sulfur dioxide (causes acid rain), and particulates (causes health problems).
  • Reducing Emissions: Use of renewable energy, carbon capture and storage, improving energy efficiency, and public transport initiatives.
