üîã Energy

  • Energy Transfers: Energy cannot be created or destroyed ‚Äî only transferred or stored. Key stores include thermal, kinetic, gravitational, elastic, and chemical.
  • Energy Efficiency: Efficiency = (Useful output √∑ Total input) √ó 100. Reduce energy loss through insulation, lubrication, or streamlining.
  • Power and Work: Power = Energy √∑ Time. Work done = Force √ó Distance moved. Both measured in joules or watts.
  • Specific Heat Capacity: Energy = mass √ó SHC √ó temperature change. Describes how much energy is needed to heat a substance.

‚ö° Electricity

  • Charge and Current: Current (I) is the flow of charge. Q = I √ó t. A higher current means more charge flows per second.
  • Voltage and Resistance: V = I √ó R. Resistance slows current flow; adding components in series increases total resistance.
  • Series and Parallel: Series: same current, shared voltage. Parallel: same voltage, shared current.
  • National Grid: Uses step-up transformers to increase voltage and reduce current ‚Äî this minimises energy loss as heat.

üå°Ô∏è Particle Model of Matter

  • States of Matter: Solids, liquids, and gases differ by particle arrangement and energy. Heating increases internal energy.
  • Specific Latent Heat: The energy needed to change state without changing temperature (e.g., melting or boiling).
  • Density: Density = mass √∑ volume. Solid objects have higher density due to closely packed particles.
  • Gas Pressure: Caused by particle collisions. Boyle‚Äôs Law: Pressure √ó Volume = constant (at constant temperature).

‚ò¢Ô∏è Atomic Structure

  • Structure of the Atom: Atoms consist of a nucleus (protons and neutrons) and orbiting electrons. Protons = atomic number.
  • Isotopes and Decay: Isotopes have the same protons, different neutrons. Radioactive decay emits alpha, beta, or gamma radiation.
  • Half-Life: The time it takes for half the radioactive nuclei to decay. Used in dating and medical applications.
  • Nuclear Equations: Show changes in mass and atomic number after radiation is emitted (e.g., alpha decay reduces both).

üèÉ Forces

  • Types of Forces: Contact (e.g. friction) and non-contact (e.g. gravity). Weight = mass √ó gravitational field strength.
  • Resultant Force: The overall force when multiple forces act on an object. Causes acceleration (F = m √ó a).
  • Motion Graphs: Distance‚Äìtime and velocity‚Äìtime graphs help describe motion. The gradient = speed or acceleration.
  • Stopping Distances: Affected by speed, road conditions, and driver reaction time. Thinking + braking distance = total stopping distance.
  • Momentum: Momentum = mass √ó velocity. Conserved in collisions and explosions.

üåä Waves

  • Wave Types: Transverse (e.g. light) and longitudinal (e.g. sound). All waves transfer energy, not matter.
  • Wave Equation: Wave speed = frequency √ó wavelength (v = f √ó Œª). Measure using a ripple tank or oscilloscope.
  • EM Spectrum: Ranges from radio waves to gamma rays. Used in communication, medicine, and security.
  • Reflection & Refraction: Waves change direction at boundaries. Light slows and bends when entering denser materials.
  • Sound Waves: Require a medium to travel. Can reflect (echo), refract, and diffract.

üß≤ Magnetism & Electromagnetism

  • Magnets: Have north and south poles. Like poles repel, opposites attract. Magnetic field lines go north to south.
  • Electromagnets: Made by running current through a wire coil. Stronger with more turns or current.
  • Motor Effect: A wire in a magnetic field experiences a force. F = B √ó I √ó L. Use Fleming‚Äôs left-hand rule.
  • Generators: Move a wire in a magnetic field to induce a current (generator effect). Basis of dynamos and alternators.
  • Transformers: Change voltage using coils of wire. Step-up (more turns in secondary), step-down (fewer turns).

üåå Space Physics (Triple Only)

  • Life Cycle of Stars: Nebula ‚Üí Protostar ‚Üí Main Sequence ‚Üí Red Giant/Supergiant ‚Üí White Dwarf / Supernova / Black Hole.
  • Red Shift: Light from distant galaxies is stretched ‚Äî evidence that the universe is expanding.
  • The Big Bang Theory: Universe started from a very small, hot, dense point and has been expanding ever since.
  • Orbits: Satellites stay in orbit due to the balance between gravity and their forward motion.

üî¨ Required Practicals (Physics)

  • Energy Transfers: Investigate effectiveness of thermal insulators using beakers and lids.
  • Specific Heat Capacity: Use immersion heater in a block with a thermometer and joulemeter.
  • Resistance: Measure current and voltage across a wire at different lengths.
  • IV Characteristics: Plot I‚ÄìV graphs for a resistor, filament bulb, and diode.
  • Density: Use a eureka can or displacement method for irregular solids.
  • Motion (Forces): Use light gates and trolleys to measure acceleration and resultant force.
  • Waves: Use a ripple tank or string to measure wave frequency, wavelength, and speed.
  • Radiation and Cooling: Compare emission of infrared from different surfaces using Leslie‚Äôs cube.

üìò Differences Between GCSE Physics Exam Boards

  • AQA: Clear structure and direct command words. Focuses heavily on practical applications and includes specific "required practicals." Emphasises calculation and explanation questions.
  • Edexcel (Pearson): Two options: Edexcel Combined Science and Separate Science Physics. Lots of emphasis on interpretation of graphs, diagrams, and data. Clear formula sheet provided in exams.
  • OCR Gateway: Modular layout (P1, P2, etc.). Applies physics to real-world contexts and often frames questions around current technology. Good for students who prefer context-led content.
  • OCR 21st Century: Less common. More thematic and less modular. Focuses on science in society and how science is developed and applied.
  • WJEC Eduqas: Used mainly in Wales. Includes extended writing and slightly more focus on sustainability and ethical issues in science. The question style often blends multiple topics.
  • Content Similarities: All boards cover the same core topics: energy, electricity, particles, atomic structure, forces, waves, magnetism, and (for Triple) space physics.
  • Key Differences: Mainly in how content is assessed, how practicals are described, and the types of extended response or data-handling questions included.
