GCSE: History

📜 History Top Tips

  • Create Timelines: Visualise chronology for key periods and events.
  • Use PEEL or PEELC for Essays: Add context and causation wherever possible.
  • Memorise Key Facts: Names, dates, places, and outcomes are regularly tested.
  • Practise Source Evaluation: Learn to identify provenance and reliability fast.
  • Link Causes and Consequences: Always go beyond just describing what happened.

📜 GCSE History

  • Know Your Exam Board Topics: Whether it’s AQA, Edexcel, OCR or WJEC, be clear on which periods and depth studies you're being assessed on (e.g. Elizabethan England, Weimar Germany, Cold War, Medicine Through Time).
  • Master Chronology: Build timelines for each topic so you understand cause and consequence, turning points, and continuity and change over time.
  • Revise Key Themes: Government and power, religion, war, society, health, and economy are common threads — think synoptically.
  • Know Key Individuals and Events: Be confident discussing people like Elizabeth I, Hitler, Hippocrates, or Stalin, and events like the Berlin Crisis, Spanish Armada, or NHS formation.
  • Practise Source Skills: Analyse provenance (origin, purpose), reliability, utility, and inference. Look for content + contextual knowledge.
  • Use PEE or PEEL Paragraphs: Make clear Point, give Evidence, Explain, and Link back to the question. Use this for essays and source responses.
  • Understand Second-Order Concepts: Causation, significance, change/continuity, and similarity/difference underpin all exam questions — know how to handle them.
  • Use Practice Questions: Practise under timed conditions using mark schemes — especially for 12-, 16-, and 20-mark questions.
  • Mind Maps and Flashcards Help: Use these for revising facts, quotes, and thematic overviews quickly and repeatedly.
  • Don’t Ignore the Historic Environment (if included): Be prepared to link facts and sources with a site-specific question — often worth 16 marks.

📜 GCSE History – Core Units

Each unit below includes key events, individuals, policies, and turning points that are essential for top grades. Focus on chronology, causation, and context in your answers.

👑 Elizabethan England (1558–1603)

  • Key Themes: Elizabeth's religious settlement, the problem of marriage and succession, threats from Mary Queen of Scots and Spain, education, and exploration.
  • Major Events: Spanish Armada (1588), Northern Rebellion (1569), execution of Mary Queen of Scots (1587), Essex Rebellion (1601).
  • Historic Environment (e.g. Globe Theatre): Understand the context of Elizabethan entertainment and how it reflected the values of the age.
  • Dates to Know: 1559 (Religious Settlement), 1570 (Papal Bull of Excommunication), 1587 (Mary executed), 1588 (Armada).

🇩🇪 Weimar and Nazi Germany (1918–1939)

  • Weimar Strengths and Weaknesses: Constitution, early challenges (Kapp Putsch, Spartacists, Ruhr crisis), and Stresemann’s recovery.
  • Rise of Hitler: 1923 (Munich Putsch), 1929 (Wall Street Crash), 1933 (Chancellor), 1934 (Night of the Long Knives).
  • Nazi Control: Gestapo, propaganda, censorship, Hitler Youth, women’s roles, education, and Nazi economic policies.
  • Opposition: White Rose group, Edelweiss Pirates, army resistance, passive resistance.

☢️ The Cold War (1945–1991)

  • Origins: Yalta and Potsdam Conferences, ideological tensions, division of Germany and Berlin.
  • Key Crises: Berlin Blockade (1948–49), Cuban Missile Crisis (1962), Hungarian Uprising (1956), Prague Spring (1968).
  • Leadership and Policies: Truman Doctrine, Marshall Aid, Khrushchev’s Peaceful Coexistence, Reagan’s Star Wars, Gorbachev's reforms.
  • Fall of Communism: Solidarity movement, fall of Berlin Wall (1989), dissolution of the USSR (1991).

🩺 Medicine Through Time (c.1250–present)

  • Medieval Period: Galen and Hippocrates, Four Humours, Black Death (1348), religious explanations for disease.
  • Renaissance and Enlightenment: Vesalius, Harvey, the printing press, Great Plague (1665), scientific observation.
  • Industrial Revolution: Jenner’s vaccination (1796), Snow’s cholera maps (1854), public health reform.
  • Modern Medicine: Pasteur’s Germ Theory, antibiotics (Fleming), the NHS (1948), DNA discovery (1953).
  • Themes Over Time: Government, science, war, and the role of the individual in medical advances.

🛡️ Norman England (1066–1100)

  • Key Events: Battle of Hastings (1066), Harrying of the North (1069–70), Domesday Book (1086), rebellion suppression.
  • Control and Government: Feudal system, castle building, Church reform, and use of sheriffs and courts.
  • Religion and Culture: Importance of cathedrals, monastic life, and Norman influence on the English Church.
  • Law and Order: Forest laws, trial by ordeal, and the development of legal systems under Norman rule.

🇷🇺 Superpower Relations and the Cold War (1941–1991)

  • Early Tensions: Grand Alliance conferences (Tehran, Yalta, Potsdam), ideological conflict, and the Iron Curtain speech (1946).
  • Cold War Crises: Berlin Crisis (1948–49), Korean War (1950–53), Cuban Missile Crisis (1962), Vietnam War escalation.
  • Détente and Tensions: SALT treaties, Helsinki Accords (1975), Afghanistan invasion (1979), Reagan’s ‘Evil Empire’ speech.
  • End of Cold War: Gorbachev’s glasnost and perestroika, fall of Berlin Wall (1989), USSR collapse (1991).
